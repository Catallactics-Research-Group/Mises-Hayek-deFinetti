# Introduction 

We strongly agree with Nobel laureate James M. Buchanan: ***arbitrage has not become central to economics as it should be!*** This paper is fundamentally about arbitrage. At the intellectual level we deliver a literal triangular arbitrage by examining and combining three separate research programs unified by a single underlying principle. That principle itself is arbitrage. We call this *arbitrage trinitarianism*. The Journal of Economic Perspectives describes its own scholarly mission as providing "intellectual arbitrage" for economists. We propose that our project should be of inherent interest to readers of JEP! 

In his 1979 book on economic methodology James M. Buchanan states unambiguously and provacatively: 

> **"Economics is about arbitrage.** *The behavioral paradigm central to economics is that of the trader whose Smithean propensity to truck and barter locates and creates opportunities for mutual gains. This paradigm is contrasted with that of the maximizing engineer who allocates scarce resources among alternatives.... the maximization paradigm is the fatal methodological flaw in modern economics."* 

Austrian catallactics, Bruno de Finetti's operational subjective probability theory, and modern finance each independently are founded upon the arbitrage principle. But these foundations in each subfield were developed in isolation of eachother -- they have not properly recognized each other as kindred. This is not a mere academic coincidence. It is an intellectual convergence to be exploited. A synoptic reading of each literature reveals these deep connections and opportunities for profound insight. This is arbitrage trinitarianism: the unification of these three separate literatures and the new insights that emerge from their combination that neither could achieve alone. This is not a simple literature survey; it is a genuinely deep intellectual triangular arbitrage to be exploited for the development of a new economic methodology. This is computational statistical catallactics!

## Substantive Suggestions

**On the epigraph:** The first sentence of the Buchanan quote ("There has been a tremendous neglect...") is doing less work than the second. Since you're using the full Buchanan quote again in the body, consider whether the epigraph should just be the second sentence — the arbitrage line — and let it stand alone with maximum impact.

**On the bold/italic emphasis:** You're leaning on it heavily — ***arbitrage has not become central to economics as it should be!*** with bold, italics, and an exclamation point is three emphasis signals at once. Pick one. The exclamation point alone probably suffices, or drop it and let the italics carry it. Over-emphasis undercuts the confident tone you're otherwise sustaining.

**On this sentence:** *"Austrian catallactics, Bruno de Finetti's operational subjective probability theory, and modern finance each independently are founded upon the arbitrage principle. But these foundations in each subfield were developed in isolation of each other"* — the phrase "in each subfield" is redundant given the preceding sentence already named the three. Cut it: *"...but these foundations were developed in isolation of each other."*

**On the final sentence:** *"This is computational statistical catallactics!"* — the exclamation point makes it feel like a punchline rather than a destination. Consider a period, or better yet, a brief clause that gestures at what computational statistical catallactics *does*, so the reader doesn't land on an unfamiliar term with no foothold. Something like: *"This is computational statistical catallactics — a new methodology that makes the unified framework empirically operational."*

**On the JEP mirror:** The observation that JEP describes itself as providing "intellectual arbitrage" is your best rhetorical move, but right now it's slightly buried mid-paragraph. Consider giving it its own sentence with a bit more room to breathe before moving on.
